What's your exam score mean?

Doctors use the American College of Radiology Breast Imaging Reporting and Data System (BI-RADS) to communicate your risk of breast cancer. There are six categories used to classify mammograms.

A zero score can represent any of the four scenarios shown in yellow. If we saw one or more of these in your scan, we will need to perform another mammogram to make sure the finiding isn't cancer.

0 - Inconclusive (mass)

There is a mass in your breast(s), which is often a non-cancer finding such as a cyst or fibroadenoma.

0 - Inconclusive (calcification)

There are calcifications in your breast(s), which are often a non-cancer finding.

0 - Inconclusive (asymmetry)

There is an asymmetry in your breast(s), which is often a non-cancer finding such as overlapping breast tissue.

0 - Inconclusive (architectural distortion)

There is architectual distortion in your breast(s), which can be due to prior surgery or other non-cancer reasons.

1 - Normal

Your breasts are completely normal, and there is no evidence of breast cancer.

2 - No evidence of breast cancer

There were findings in your scan, but none of them represent cancer.

Bilateral Screening Mammogram

This type of mammogram is performed to check for signs of cancer in women who don’t have any symptoms or breast problems. The test usually consists of four x-ray pictures (a top-view and a side-view for each breast).

Diagnostic Mammogram

When a patient has breast problems or has something abnormal on a screening mammogram, a diagnostic mammogram is needed. This usually consists of more x-ray pictures to take a closer look at any problem areas.

Indication

The Indication section is included in most radiology studies and typically states the reason why your doctor ordered this particular test. Often there will be billing code information included in this section.

About breast density

Breasts are made of fat and gland tissue. The amount of fat and gland tissue in the breast determines how the breast will look on a mammogram. The more gland tissue in a breast, the more white the breast appears on a mammogram.

The amount of fat and gland tissue in the breast is graded from 1 to 4, all of which are normal. 1 means mostly fat tissue. 4 means mostly gland tissue.

Your breast score is 2, which means that there is slightly more fat than gland tissue. This is normal.

Describing Location: Quadrants

The breast is mentally divided into four sections by the radiologist to easily locate things within it. The upper outer quadrant is the section closest to the armpit and shoulder.

Describing location: The Clock

To describe the location of a finding more specifically, the face of a clock is mentally imagined on the breast with the center of the clock over the nipple.

Describing location: Depth

To describe where something is inside the breast, the radiologist mentally divides the breast into three levels of depth from front to back:

Anterior depth - near the front/nipple

Middle depth - about halfway between the nipple and the rib cage

Posterior depth - in the deepest third of the breast tissue, closest to the rib cage

Calcifications

These mineral deposits (of calcium) in the breast are common in women, and can be caused by many different things. Sometimes they are casued by breast cancer.

The shape and amount of calcification can sometimes help a radiologist tell whether or not a calcification is cancer. Abnormal calcifications may require a biopsy to determine if they are cancer.

Suspicious/worrisome

These terms are used to describe findings that are not normal and may represent cancer.

Mass

A collection or lump of tissue that takes up space. While cancer can cause a mass, other things that are not cancer can also take the shape of a mass.

Asymmetry

Breast tissue normally appears similar in the right breast and the left breast. An area of tissue that stands out when compared to the other breast is called an asymmetry. While cancer can cause an asymmetry, other things that are not cancer can also cause asymmetry.

Equal density

The mass is a similar shade of gray on the mammogram compared to normal surrounding breast tissue.

Oval

This describes the shape of a mass. Oval masses are typically not cancer. It is rare for cancer to appear as an oval mass on a mammogram.

Circumscribed

This describes the borders of a mass. Circumscribed means that the mass has well-defined borders and edges. Circumscribed masses are usually not cancer.

Pleomorphic

A term used to describe calcifications that are small and are varied in shape. This type of calcification is sometimes associated with breast cancer.
